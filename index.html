what just happened

The correlation is real. You can pull the numbers from Tyler Vigen's archive — the r-values are around 0.9 and the data is sourced.

What's fake is the sentence that connected it to your answers. There's no dataset of coffee drinkers and cheese consumption. That sentence was written to see what your mind would do with it.

Most people do the same thing: a small explanation starts forming. Coffee drinkers might be... You don't choose to build it. It arrives mostly assembled, and it feels like reasoning.

This is post-hoc rationalization. The conclusion forms first, fast, often from cues you didn't consciously register. The reasoning is generated afterward and presented in an order that makes it look causal. From the inside, the two steps feel like one.

It runs most decisions. The useful part isn't avoiding it — you can't. It's recognizing the shape of it when it happens, so you can ask whether the reason you're giving is the reason you actually have.

Why this matters right now. A lot of what currently passes for disagreement is two people defending conclusions they reached before the conversation started. A lot of what currently passes for research is sorting through information until something confirms a position the gut already took. And a lot of what currently feels like clear thinking — your own included — is reasoning that was generated to support a decision, not to make one.

You can't fix this in other people. You can sometimes catch it in yourself, if you know what it looks like. Now you've seen it once.
